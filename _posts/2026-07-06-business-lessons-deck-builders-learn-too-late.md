Building a great deck and building a great deck *business* are two very different skill sets. Many contractors spend years mastering joist spans and railing details while the business side — licensing, taxes, permits, pricing — gets figured out through trial, error, and the occasional expensive mistake. Here's a rundown of the foundational lessons that tend to separate a struggling solo builder from a thriving, sustainable deck-building company.

### Start With Business Basics, Not Just Tool Skills

A local community college business class is one of the most overlooked investments a new contractor can make. It covers the fundamentals of running any business — the administrative and financial side that rarely shows up in hands-on training but ends up determining whether a company survives its first few years.

### Get Professional Help With Taxes Early

Filing taxes and payroll without professional support is a common early-career habit that tends to catch up with a business. Late filings can escalate from occasional missed deadlines to more frequent required filings and direct attention from state revenue departments. Bringing in an accountant or CPA — even though it involves giving them visibility into business accounts and expense categories — removes this burden entirely and keeps filings accurate and on time.

The broader lesson: successful builders tend to focus their energy on the parts of the business they're best at, and hire out the rest.

### Learn the Trade From the Ground Up First

For anyone starting out, spending a year or two working for an established company can be more valuable than jumping straight into solo contracting. This is the stage to absorb everything — footing layouts, concrete pours, screw pile systems, trim and railing work — and to keep a written record of what's learned on each job. It's also a chance to shadow the sales side of the business: going out on estimates, seeing how projects are priced and sold, and understanding the full lifecycle of a job before running one independently.

### Build a Team, Not Just a Solo Operation

Working alone is possible, but it becomes physically demanding over time and limits how much work can be taken on. A small crew — one or two employees in addition to the owner — significantly increases output and efficiency. Larger crews (six or seven person teams) can move fast, but they also risk inefficiency if there isn't enough work to keep everyone productive at once. A three- or four-person team tends to hit a sweet spot for handling a mid-sized deck project efficiently.

### Treat Your Web Presence Like a Core Business Function

A properly indexed website, an active Google Business profile, and consistent social media posting are treated as essential infrastructure rather than optional extras. Sporadic posting doesn't build an audience — consistency (ideally daily, or at minimum three to four times per week) is what keeps followers engaged and growing. Cross-linking social content back to a website, and periodically checking that all links still function, rounds out a solid digital presence. For anyone who isn't naturally web-savvy, bringing in outside help for web maintenance or marketing is a reasonable investment.

### Licensing, Bonding, and Insurance Aren't Optional

Operating as a contractor typically requires three things working in tandem:

- **A local business license** for the jurisdiction being worked in
- **A state contractor's license**
- **A bond and insurance policy**

The bond protects the client if workmanship issues arise, and insurance covers the contractor in the event of a major mistake or damage. This is also the core reason licensed, bonded contractors charge more than unlicensed labor — the protections in place for both the client and the business justify the cost. Attempting unfamiliar techniques on a client's property without disclosing the risk is a practice to avoid; any uncertainty about whether a method will work should be communicated clearly upfront.

### Permits, Site Plans, and Structural Engineering

Pulling a building permit is one of the more technical hurdles in this line of work, especially once a structure exceeds a certain height. A full permit package typically includes construction drawings covering the overhead, plan, side, and front views, along with a plot plan.

Plot plans in particular can be time-consuming to produce manually — one described case took an hour and a half to complete without outside help. A paid site-plan service (referenced at a cost of $150) can place a house on its lot with accurate dimensions, saving significant time compared to producing one from scratch.

A structural engineer is equally essential to this process. Engineers handle the math behind member sizing — hole depth and width for concrete footings, post and beam sizing, and how far a joist can span to a beam. The typical workflow: construction drawings go to the engineer for markup, revisions are made to address any concerns, and the engineer stamps and signs the final packet before it's submitted with the permit application.

### Learn to Read Blueprints

Bidding on blueprinted jobs — where a contractor or client provides an existing set of drawings — requires the ability to read and interpret those plans. Blueprint quality varies widely: some are simple and easy to follow, while others (particularly detailed commercial sets) take considerable time to study and understand. Developing this skill early avoids the slow, trial-and-error process of learning to read a blueprint mid-bid.

### Price by the Item, Not by the Square Foot

Flat per-square-foot pricing (a commonly used shortcut) can leave money on the table if it isn't broken down carefully. An itemized approach — pricing decking, fascia, surface, borders, skirting, steps, railing, lighting, and patio covers separately — makes it easier to account for every cost and to justify the final number to a client, line by line. Comparing pricing against competitors periodically also helps ensure a business's rates stay aligned with its market and its own margin needs.
