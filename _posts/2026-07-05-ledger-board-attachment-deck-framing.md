The ledger board is the single point where a deck attaches to the house — and it's also the most common failure point in deck construction. Getting it right comes down to three decisions: the material you choose, whether you pre-install your joist hangers, and what an inspector will be looking for when they show up. Here's how to think through each one.

### Choosing Your Ledger Material

Not all ledger board materials behave the same way once they're on the wall. The options generally fall into a few categories:

- **Standard pressure-treated lumber** (in some regions, pressure-treated hemlock) is the industry-standard baseline material.
- **Kiln-dried after treatment (KDAT)** lumber is treated and then kiln-dried, which can reduce shrinkage compared to standard pressure-treated stock. It has limited availability and costs more.
- **Treated engineered lumber, such as laminated veneer lumber (LVL)** offers more dimensional consistency — pieces tend to run the same size, so there's less concern about shrinkage, expansion, and contraction. It's treated from the inside out and, in the case referenced here, is strong enough to span farther than standard treated lumber.
- **Aluminum or steel framing systems** sit at the top of the price range and represent a significant cost jump. This option won't fit every budget.

In the project referenced here, treated LVL was the material of choice specifically because it combines the treated-lumber characteristics builders expect with more dimensional consistency and greater strength than standard treated hemlock.

One practical note on engineered lumber: it's sometimes available in very long single pieces — up to 54 feet, in the case of the LVL product referenced — which can eliminate the need for splices on longer runs. In the example described, a single 34-foot piece was used to cover a 32-foot, 10-inch span.

### Pre-Hanging Joist Hangers: Why Material Choice Changes Your Process

The material you choose determines how you can install joist hangers.

**With standard pressure-treated lumber**, inconsistencies in joist thickness typically mean you can't pre-install joist hangers on the ledger ahead of time. Instead, the ledger goes up on the house first, layout is marked, and then each joist is brought to the ledger individually, temporarily tacked in place, and the hanger is installed after the fact. This process takes more time and offers less opportunity for prep work.

**With dimensionally consistent engineered lumber** (like the treated LVL used in this example), joist hangers can be pre-installed directly onto the ledger before it ever goes up on the house — because the material's consistent sizing means every hanger will land at the same, predictable depth.

It's worth noting that KDAT lumber, while kiln-dried, still isn't perfectly uniform piece to piece — some size variance between joists should still be expected. Metal framing systems, on the other hand, require every component to come from the same system; parts aren't interchangeable between manufacturers.

### Calculating Fastener Spacing

Fastener count and spacing aren't guesswork — they depend on several variables:

- The size of the deck
- The live load the deck is designed for (in pounds per square foot)
- How far the joists extend from the house
- How close together the joists are spaced on center

This kind of span-and-load data is available through fastener manufacturers' published span tables — for example, FastenMaster publishes a chart showing recommended fastener patterns (installed in a staggered "V" formation), along with minimum edge-distance requirements labeled by letter (A, B, C) for different parts of the deck.

**A worked example from this approach:** for a deck built to a 60 lb-per-square-foot live load, with an 8-foot span to the beam but a 10-foot total joist length, using LVL material (which can be treated like Douglas fir or southern yellow pine for span-table purposes, and offers even greater strength than Douglas fir), the applicable table called for a fastener every 10 inches on center, with a minimum edge distance of 3¾ inches — rounded up in practice to about 4 inches.

A few practical layout habits worth adopting:

- **Double up fasteners at both ends** of the ledger, even if the span table only calls for a single fastener at the ends.
- **Stagger fasteners high and low** along the length of the ledger rather than running them in a single line.
- **Adjust spacing near joist hanger locations** — if a standard interval would land a fastener too close to where a hanger needs to go, shift that fastener slightly (for example, using 9 inches instead of 10) to avoid a conflict.

Keep in mind that this spacing example reflects one specific deck's load and span requirements — always calculate fastener spacing based on your own deck's dimensions, live load, and the applicable fastening schedule. ### Installing Pre-Hung Joist Hangers

Once fastener locations are laid out, joist hangers can be installed using a template to keep placement consistent — flushing each hanger to the top edge of the ledger and fastening it into position. A metal-connector nail gun designed specifically for joist hanger nails can speed up this process considerably compared to installing each nail by hand. Positioning each hanger on the layout line before fully seating it helps keep the entire run straight.

For attaching joists to the wall once the ledger is up, structural drive screws (2½ inches long) are used through all four holes of each hanger.

The ledger itself attaches to the house using ledger lock fasteners, which are separate from the joist hanger fasteners.

### What Inspectors Are Looking For

When it's time for a building inspection, the inspector will check your ledger's fastener count and spacing against your approved plans. If fasteners aren't staggered correctly, or there simply aren't enough of them, that's grounds for a failed inspection — the inspector may require additional fasteners before signing off. Even a single missing nail in a joist hanger can be enough to hold up final approval.

The takeaway: match your fastener layout to your plans exactly, fill every hole in every joist hanger, and use the correct fastener type throughout. Getting this fundamental right the first time avoids callbacks — getting it wrong means a return trip to fix it before you can close out the permit.
