Building a deck frame that stays flat and square comes down to a handful of details most people never think about until something goes wrong. Beam splicing, joist height variation, and blocking placement are the unglamorous steps that make the difference between a clean, professional-looking deck and one with visible flaws in the surface. Here's a practical breakdown of how to frame a pressure-treated deck correctly, from the beams up.

### Planning Your Beam Spans

Dimensional lumber typically tops out at 20 feet in length, so if your deck span is wider than that — say, close to 23 feet — you'll need to splice beams together. The key detail here: **plan your splice location so it lands directly on top of a footing**. This isn't optional or cosmetic; it's a structural necessity that keeps the joint properly supported.

For wider spans between footings, doubled-up 2x12s can give you more span capacity than a single beam size would allow.

A practical build tip: assemble your beams on the ground first, then carry the fully built beam into place rather than trying to fasten it together while it's already positioned. It's easier on your back and easier to keep everything aligned.

### Squaring Your Beams Without a Single Calculation

If your deck has an inside corner against the house, there's a no-math way to get your beams perfectly square:

- **Run a string line** from the inside corner of the house out to the beam
- **Bring the line to the outside edge** of the beam — it should be even with the house line
- **Cross-check with a diagonal measurement** across the beams; if both diagonals match, you're square

This method holds even if the house itself isn't perfectly square, because you're squaring off the house line rather than assuming a theoretical perfect right angle. It's a simple trick, but it saves a lot of second-guessing during layout.

### Handling Natural Variation in Joist Height

Because dimensional lumber is a natural product, joist heights can vary by as much as a quarter inch from piece to piece — even within a batch labeled the same nominal size. A 2x8, for example, might measure anywhere from about 7¼" to 7½".

The fix is simple but often overlooked:

- **Measure every joist individually** before installation
- **Sort them into piles** by actual height (e.g., 7½", 7⅜", 7¼")
- **Install them in a graduated order** — largest to smallest, or in a consistent progression — rather than randomly

Skipping this step is a common cause of a bumpy or uneven deck surface. Alternating a 7¼" joist next to a 7½" joist and then back down creates visible high and low spots across the decking. Sorting first keeps the transition gradual and the surface smooth.

### Installing Joists on a Free-Standing Deck

For a free-standing deck (one not directly ledger-attached to the house), the rim joist goes in first, set right on top of the beam closest to the house. From there, joists are stood up and nailed in one at a time. On larger decks, pushing each joist against the house wall as you work gives you clear access to nail from behind — a small workflow adjustment that makes a repetitive task go faster.

When you're not yet sure where each joist will land after installation, it's common practice to run the joist stock long, set everything in place, then measure and mark the cut line from the house on both sides once the layout is confirmed. Snapping a chalk line across those marks and cutting all joists to it in one pass keeps the entire row perfectly straight — no repeated measuring or guesswork per board.

### Don't Skip the Blocking

Blocking between joists is a small step with an outsized payoff:

- **Reduces movement** in the deck frame over time
- **Distributes structural load** more evenly between adjacent joists
- **Stiffens the overall frame**

On one example build with joists spaced 12 inches on center, the blocking pieces were pre-cut to 10.5 inches to fit snugly between them. That specific dimension applies to that layout — the correct blocking length for your own deck will depend on your actual joist spacing and lumber dimensions, so measure your own on-center spacing and joist thickness rather than assuming a fixed number. Installing blocking between each row of joists is an inexpensive step relative to the stability it adds.

### Choosing Lumber Treated for Ground Contact

Not all pressure-treated lumber carries the same level of protection. Some suppliers treat all of their dimensional lumber to a ground-contact rating as standard, which matters most on decks built low to the ground, where framing members sit closer to soil moisture and debris. Even on decks that aren't low to the ground, ground-contact-rated lumber carries extra preservative. If you're comparing lumber options, checking whether a supplier rates their stock for ground contact — rather than the more limited standard treatment — removes some of the guesswork from durability.

### The Bottom Line

None of these techniques are complicated on their own — a string line, a tape measure, a chalk line. But together they add up to a deck frame that's flat, square, and properly supported. Getting the planning stage right, sorting your joists, and not skipping the blocking are small investments of time with a real payoff in the finished structure.
